## Challenge

Midwestern State University had accumulated over 120 linear feet of unprocessed administrative records from their Office of the President spanning 1965-2005. The materials were stored in original file folders and banker's boxes with minimal organization, making them essentially inaccessible to researchers. The university needed these records processed to support their upcoming centennial celebration and ongoing institutional research needs.

The collection included correspondence, reports, meeting minutes, planning documents, and photographic materials. Many records contained sensitive information requiring careful access restrictions. The university's timeline was tight - they needed finding aids completed within five months.

## Approach

After an initial survey, we recommended a MPLP (More Product, Less Process) approach focused on series-level description with folder-level detail where materials warranted closer attention. This balanced the university's need for timely access against their budget constraints.

We proposed:
- Minimal refoldering focused on preservation concerns
- Series-level arrangement reflecting original office structure
- Folder-level inventory for high-value materials
- Separation and appropriate handling of restricted materials
- EAD-encoded finding aid for online publication
- Basic digital surrogate creation for heavily-used materials

## Process

Our team began with a detailed survey and arrangement plan, identifying seven major series:

1. Correspondence Files (1965-2005)
2. Board of Trustees Records (1968-2004)
3. Strategic Planning Documents (1975-2005)
4. President's Reports and Speeches (1965-2005)
5. Campus Development and Facilities (1970-2000)
6. Student Affairs and Campus Life (1965-1995)
7. Photographic Materials (1965-2005)

We maintained the original filing system where it was logical, intervening only where disorder impeded access. Materials were rehoused in acid-free folders and archival boxes with clear, consistent labeling.

Restricted materials - primarily personnel files and student records - were identified, separated, and documented with specific access restrictions following university policy and FERPA requirements.

The finding aid was developed in compliance with DACS standards and encoded in EAD for integration with the university's ArchivesSpace instance.

## Outcomes

**Deliverables:**
- 120 linear feet processed and rehoused in archival materials
- Comprehensive finding aid with 847 folder-level descriptions
- EAD-encoded finding aid published in university's online catalog
- Separated restricted materials with clear access documentation
- Processing manual documenting decisions and procedures
- 250+ frequently-requested items digitized for reference use

**Impact:**
The collection became immediately useful for the university's centennial research. Within the first month of availability, the archives reported 15 research requests specifically for these materials. The finding aid has been accessed over 200 times online, demonstrating strong researcher interest.

The university archivist noted: "This project transformed an unusable backlog into one of our most-consulted collections. The clear documentation and online finding aid have made these materials discoverable to researchers across the country."
