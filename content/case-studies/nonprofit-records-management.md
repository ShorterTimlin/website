## Challenge

Community Action Partnership, a nonprofit serving low-income families for over 50 years, faced an archival crisis when a pipe burst damaged their record storage area. Approximately 35 linear feet of historical program records, grant files, and client documentation were affected by water damage.

The organization needed:
- Rapid assessment of damage and salvageable materials
- Emergency stabilization and recovery
- Processing of recovered materials with proper arrangement
- Development of ongoing records management system
- Compliance documentation for grant reporting requirements
- Historical documentation for their 50th anniversary publication

Time was critical - damaged materials were deteriorating, and the organization needed specific records for active grant reporting.

## Approach

We implemented an emergency response and recovery plan:

**Immediate Response (Week 1)**
- On-site damage assessment within 48 hours
- Priority identification for critical operational records
- Emergency stabilization of wet materials
- Coordination with restoration specialists for severe damage

**Recovery Phase (Weeks 2-4)**
- Controlled drying and cleaning of affected materials
- Triage between salvageable and non-salvageable items
- Documentation of losses for insurance purposes
- Digital capture of at-risk but stable materials

**Processing Phase (Weeks 5-12)**
- Arrangement of recovered and existing records
- Integration of digital surrogates where originals were lost
- Finding aid development
- Records management system design

**Sustainability Phase (Ongoing)**
- Staff training on records management
- Policy development for retention and storage
- Disaster preparedness planning

## Process

Our initial assessment found that approximately 60% of the affected records were salvageable with proper treatment. We prioritized materials in this order:

1. Active grant files needed for current reporting (immediate digitization)
2. Irreplaceable historical materials (program photographs, founding documents)
3. Client case files requiring retention for legal/funding compliance
4. Historical program records documenting impact and activities
5. Administrative files with archival value

We worked closely with the organization's development director to identify which grant files were urgently needed, scanning those materials immediately even before full processing.

The recovered collection was organized into four major series:

1. Organizational Records (1973-2023) - Board minutes, policies, strategic planning
2. Program Records (1973-2023) - Documentation of services and client outcomes
3. Grant and Funding Files (1973-2023) - Proposals, reports, compliance documentation
4. Community Impact Documentation (1973-2023) - Photographs, publications, media coverage

We developed a simple but effective records management system including:
- Clear retention schedules aligned with legal requirements
- Defined transfer procedures from active to archival storage
- Environmental monitoring for storage areas
- Digital backup procedures for critical materials

## Outcomes

**Immediate Results:**
- 22 linear feet successfully recovered and stabilized
- All active grant files digitized and returned to operation within 2 weeks
- Insurance documentation completed for destroyed materials
- Critical compliance records preserved and accessible

**Long-term Deliverables:**
- Comprehensive finding aid for organizational archives
- Records management manual and retention schedule
- Staff training for 8 employees
- Disaster preparedness plan
- Environmental monitoring system for storage areas
- Digital repository for high-value materials

**Impact:**
The organization successfully met all grant reporting deadlines despite the crisis. The recovered historical materials supported their 50th anniversary book, which highlighted program evolution and community impact.

The Executive Director noted: "When disaster struck, we thought we'd lost our history. Not only did Shorter Timlin save most of our records, but they helped us build a system to protect them going forward. We're better organized now than before the incident."

The project also led to a successful NHPRC grant application for continued archival work, which we supported through the application process.
